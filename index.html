Why Donki Conquered Singapore But Stumbled in Malaysia

Experiential retail alone is not enough. International success demands alignment between localisation, pricing, and expansion strategy with each country's economic and cultural reality.

01 — Introduction

What is Don Don Donki?

Don Don Donki is a Japanese discount retail store that sells a wide range of products, including groceries, snacks, cosmetics, and household items. It is known for its Japanese imports and lively, crowded shopping environment, accompanied by a catchy in-store song. Operated by Pan Pacific International Holdings, the chain has expanded internationally, with stores in Japan, Singapore, Hong Kong, Macau, Thailand, Taiwan, Malaysia, and parts of the United States.

Don Don Donki originated from Japan's Don Quijote chain, which was founded in 1980, and later expanded overseas under the “Don Don Donki” brand. The company entered Southeast Asia in 2017 with its first store in Singapore, and has since rapidly expanded across Asia, including Hong Kong, Thailand, Taiwan, and Malaysia. In Singapore, its first outlet opened in Orchard Central in 2017 and the chain has grown steadily, becoming one of its most successful international markets with multiple stores and strong consumer demand. In Malaysia, Don Don Donki launched its first store in Kuala Lumpur in 2021 (under the “JONETZ” branding), marking further regional expansion, though its performance has been more mixed compared to Singapore.

02 — Argument One

Experiential Retail:
When "Treasure Hunt" Works — and When It Doesn't

Experiential retail refers to a store design philosophy that prioritises sensory engagement, discovery, and emotional stimulation over transactional efficiency. Rather than organising products logically for quick purchase, experiential retailers deliberately create complexity through densely packed shelves, unexpected product adjacencies, and stimulating visuals and sounds to encourage extended dwell time, impulse purchases, and repeat visits.

Donki's version of this, sometimes called the "compressed display" or "treasure hunt" model, is central to its brand identity. The question is not whether this model works in principle — it clearly does in Japan. The question is under what cultural and consumer conditions it transfers successfully.

✓ SUCCESS

Singapore: Controlled Chaos as Entertainment

🏙️
Urban Consumer Profile

Singapore's highly urbanised, internationally-exposed consumer base has significant experience with diverse retail formats. Such as Japanese convenience stores, department stores, and specialty retailers. This familiarity reduced the mental strain of navigating Donki's dense layouts.

🎌
Japanese Culture

Singapore has a well-established cultural affinity for Japanese products, pop culture (anime, manga, J-pop), and food. This familiarity is reflected in tourism patterns: in 2025, 726,200 people travelled from Singapore to Japan, compared to around 636,000 from Malaysia despite Malaysia’s much larger population. This made Donki’s Japanese retail experience feel more familiar and appealing to Singaporean consumers.

📱
Social Media Virality

The store's unique aesthetic including bright lights, packed shelves, the Donki penguin mascot was proved highly "trendy". Donki being shared on social media increased repeat visits and tourist footfall, particularly at the iconic Orchard Central outlet.

🔁
Repeat Visit Culture

Singaporean consumers reported enjoying the unpredictability of Donki. For example: the idea that you may find something different each visit. This aligned with the treasure hunt model's core value proposition: discovery as entertainment.

✗ STRUGGLE

Malaysia: Confused and Overwhelmed customers

😵
Sensory Overload Without Familiarity

Malaysian consumers, particularly those outside Kuala Lumpur, had less prior exposure to the Japanese retail format. Without the cultural familiarity to understand Donki's layout, the dense, maze-like store design felt confusing rather than playful.

🛒
Preference for Clarity

Consumer feedback from Malaysian shoppers indicated a preference for clearer product categorisation and navigation. The deliberate complexity of Donki's layout, which is a feature in Japan and Singapore was perceived as poor store design in the Malaysian context.

🏪
Competition with Established Formats

Malaysia has well-embedded hypermarket and supermarket culture (Aeon, Lotus's/Tesco, Giant). These offer clearer navigation, halal-certified products throughout, and lower price points. The value of Donki's "experience" was harder to justify against alternatives that understood the local customer's desire better.

📉
One-Time Curiosity, Not Loyalty

While Donki's Malaysian outlets attracted initial curiosity traffic, reports indicate limited repeat visit behaviour. The "new" experience did not convert into sustained shopping habit, suggesting the experiential model failed to place itself in Malaysian consumer routine.

💡

Key Insight: Experiential retail is not universally appealing. Its success depends on the consumer's pre-existing cultural literacy of the retail format and their willingness to trade efficiency for entertainment. Singapore had this; Malaysia, broadly, did not.

03 — Argument Two

Pricing Strategy:
Same Tags, Different Worlds

Price perception is relative, not absolute. The same price point can signal "affordable quality" in one market and "unjustifiable premium" in another. Donki's pricing strategy, largely standardised across Southeast Asian markets, created fundamentally different value propositions depending on local purchasing power and competitive environment.

04 — Argument Three

Expansion & Localisation:
Getting the Details Right (or Wrong)

Beyond atmosphere and price is the practical side of localisation, which includes where stores are located, how quickly expansion proceeds, and how much decision-making power is delegated to local management. On these points, Donki's Singapore strategy was very different and more successful than its approach in Malaysia.

🕌

Halal Integration

🇸🇬

Donki adapted to local consumer needs by introducing dedicated halal-certified sections, despite Muslims forming only around 15% of the population. This recognised both Muslim consumers and wider demand for halal-certified products among non-Muslims and tourists. By doing so, Donki successfully localized its Japanese product range without weakening its brand identity.

🇲🇾

In contrast, Donki was slower and less effective in adapting to Malaysia’s Muslim-majority market, where around 63% of the population is Muslim. The limited availability of halal-certified products significantly restricted accessibility for many consumers, especially since numerous Japanese snacks contain pork-derived gelatin or alcohol flavourings. This lack of localization reduced Donki’s market appeal and contributed to weaker customer retention.

📍

Store Location Strategy

🇸🇬

Strategic placement in high-footfall, high-spending corridors: Tanjong Pagar, Orchard Central, City Square Mall. Each location was chosen for maximum exposure to young, educated, internationally-oriented consumers.

🇲🇾

While Mid Valley Megamall provided a great location, subsequent expansion decisions were criticised for misjudging demand. Locations in malls without sufficient Japanese-curious footfall reduced conversion and undermined the quality of the brand.

🏢

Local Management Autonomy

🇸🇬

PPIH granted Singapore operations significant local management autonomy, allowing product adaptation, promotional strategy, and operational decisions to be made by teams with direct market knowledge. This understanding allowed quick adaptation to local consumer feedback.

🇲🇾

Reports suggest Malaysian operations was managed by Singapore's management which reduced the ability to respond to local market signals. Without in-depth local understanding, the products in Donki were not adapted to Malaysian consumer preferences which lost many customers interest.

📈

Expansion Pace

🇸🇬

Singapore's expansion was measured and sequential. The first store opened in a high-traffic, premium location at Orchard, which helped attract strong attention. Donki did not expand too quickly and instead learned from customer feedback to understand what worked locally before opening more stores.

🇲🇾

Malaysia saw what analysts describe as premature overexpansion. In Malaysia, stores opened before the brand had achieved sufficient consumer familiarity, halal compliance, or product localisation. This diluted brand perception and did not attract new or repeat customers.

💡

Key Insight: Localisation is very significant in expanding internationally. Donki's success in Singapore was built on structural adaptation: halal sections, well-chosen locations, empowered local management, and a deliberate expansion pace. Malaysia experienced the consequences of what happens when these aspects are absent or under-resourced.

05 — Conclusion

The Limits of Experiential Retail

The comparative performance of Don Don Donki in Singapore and Malaysia offers a clear case study in the complexity of international retail expansion. At first glance, both markets received the same brand, the same store concept, and broadly the same product offering. Yet the outcomes were significantly different.

This divergence reveals a fundamental truth about global retail: experiential retail is not a self-sufficient expansion strategy. The “treasure hunt” model that made Donki a famous destination in Japan and a success in Singapore is not a transferable formula. It's effectiveness depends on cultural sensitivity, economic compatibility, and proper localisation.

Singapore provided the ideal conditions: an internationally-oriented, Japan-curious consumer base with the spending power to accept Donki's price points, embedded in a retail culture where novelty and entertainment are valued. Donki met these conditions with appropriately localised products, smart location choices, halal integration, and empowered local management.

Malaysia presented a different landscape: a more price-sensitive market, a dominant Muslim majority requiring halal compliance, established local supermarket and hypermarket competition, and consumers with less pre-existing affinity for the Japanese retail format. Donki's strategy did not adequately adapt to these realities. The result was a brand that felt foreign rather than fun, expensive rather than accessible, and complicated rather than exciting.

Core Thesis

Don Don Donki's success in Singapore and failure in Malaysia demonstrates that experiential retail alone is insufficient for international success. Sustainable cross-border retail growth requires the alignment of three interdependent aspects:

01
Experiential Design — adapted to local expereiences and consumer expectations
02
Pricing Strategy — related to local purchasing power and competitive context
03
Localisation Infrastructure — halal compliance, location selection, management autonomy, and measured expansion
